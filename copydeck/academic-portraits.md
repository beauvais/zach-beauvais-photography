## SECTION: THE PROBLEM (NO EYEBROW — RUNS DIRECTLY UNDER HEADER)

```
Was your profile photo taken at a conference, grabbed by a colleague with a
phone, or produced by the department's annual photo day with a borrowed
backdrop and a photographer who had twelve minutes per person? It doesn't
look like you — it looks like someone who was briefly photographed.

That matters more than it used to. Your image appears on your faculty page,
your Google Scholar profile, your publisher's website, your speaker bio, your
ResearchGate. It's often the first thing a prospective student, collaborator,
or journalist sees.

I spent eight years at Cornell and a good stretch before that at an agency
whose clients were almost entirely Cambridge University departments. I know
the culture, the environments, and what these images need to do — on a
faculty page, a book jacket, a conference slide, a research profile.
```

> **Notes:** Opens by naming the exact situation — no softening, but not condescending either. The list of where the image appears is doing SEO work and makes the stakes legible. "Someone who was briefly photographed" is the core contrast. Cornell and Cambridge establish cultural credibility — Purdue and U of I are named in the lead as the working locations. The two do different jobs; keep both.

## SECTION: WHAT THE SESSION LOOKS LIKE

**Eyebrow:** `How it works`
**H2:** `A conversation before the camera comes out.`

```
We start purposefully: I want to know what these images are for — a poster, an author bio,
a lab microsite, a conference keynote? What do you want it to say? How do you want to say it?

Location is part of the image. Some people want their office — books, natural
light, a sense of the actual environment where the thinking happens. Some want
somewhere more neutral. Some want outside. We figure it out together, and I'll
make suggestions if you'd like them.

The session itself is unhurried. I don't direct you into poses. We talk, we
move around, and I photograph as things happen. The portraits that come out of
that approach tend to look more like you than anything produced by stand here,
chin up, good, hold that.
```

> **Notes:** "What would you like it to not look like?" is a good question to include — it opens a real conversation and signals you're not going to produce something generic. Office setting named first because that's the natural academic instinct, but grounded without being prescriptive. Last paragraph is a light articulation of the collaborative approach without overselling it. "Stand here, chin up, good, hold that" — specific, wry, earns its place.

**On "headshots":**
Academics often search "faculty headshots" or "professor headshots." These are out of scope per standing decisions, and the word doesn't appear in copy. The page earns the same search intent through "faculty portrait," "research profile photo," and the named platforms (Google Scholar, faculty page, etc.) — users searching for headshots who land here will understand immediately what they're getting instead. Don't add "headshots" to copy; it undermines the positioning.
