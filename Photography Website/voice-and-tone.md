# Zach Beauvais — Voice & Tone Reference

*Distilled from ~179 blog posts (2007–2022). For use at the start of new chats to avoid re-analysis.*

---

## The short version

Write like a thoughtful person talking, not a marketer performing. Specific, wry, honest, sensory. No fluff. Self-aware without being self-absorbed.

---

## Voice characteristics

**First-person and immediate.** He writes from his own perspective without hedging. No "one might argue" — he says what he sees.

**Sensory and specific.** Abstract ideas arrive through concrete physical detail: the smell of birch while green-carving, the sound of paws on snow vs. boots, rain-beaded glass, the texture of windowsills. Vagueness is not his default.

**Associatively structured.** He follows the mind's natural movement — from spoon carving to politics, from a dog walk to a medieval lord, from a Glasgow coffee shop to identity. These tangents aren't digressions; they're the point. The unexpected connection is where the meaning lives.

**Self-aware about craft.** He signals what he's doing (his "Just type" posts explicitly disavow editing). He's honest about what he doesn't know and what he got wrong.

**Wry, dry humour.** British-American hybrid. Deadpan, self-deprecating. Funny because it's precise, not because it's trying to be funny. Example register: "I think not" / "They're mad as a skip of toads, of course."

**Parentheticals and dashes for texture.** He uses parentheses for asides and em-dashes for pivots. Not as structural elements — as rhythm.

**Occasional profanity, used precisely.** Swearing when it carries weight. Never decorative.

---

## Tone

**Warmly honest.** He says difficult things from a place of care. He doesn't soften to the point of dishonesty.

**Nostalgic without sentiment.** He revisits memory with precision and feeling, but doesn't wallow or editorialise excessively. "I knew who I was, where I wanted to be, and would happily live eternity sat in that chair in aspic." That's it. He doesn't explain why it matters.

**Anti-bullshit.** Consistently allergic to corporate language, marketing speak, empty content. He questions why things are made in the first place. This carries into how he talks about his own work — he won't overclaim.

**Vulnerable but stoic.** He'll write about anxiety, grief, paralysis, injury, identity crisis — but always with a degree of humour or perspective that keeps it from collapsing into self-pity.

**Craft-oriented and honest about standards.** The spoon-carving post is the clearest example: he burned spoons he'd spent hours on because they weren't good enough. He doesn't romanticise the learning curve. He says where he went wrong and what he'll do next.

---

## Structural patterns

- **Starts in media res or with a sensory hook.** Not "I want to talk about X" but the thing itself: "Paws hit the snow much faster than boots."
- **Moves from concrete/personal → broader/universal.** Grounds the abstract in lived experience before opening it out.
- **Short punchy sentences for emphasis; longer ones for texture.** He uses rhythm consciously, though informally.
- **Rhetorical questions embedded in prose.** Not bolded headers with question marks — questions that move the thought forward.
- **Ends on something earned.** Often a twist on an earlier image, or a pithy line that snaps the piece closed.

---

## For photography specifically

His sensory writing maps directly to visual work. He already describes light, place, and atmosphere with photographer's precision — he just does it in words. When writing about his photography work, the register should feel like someone who:
- Is genuinely interested in what the subject does to him, not what it looks like on paper
- Is honest about what makes an image work and what doesn't
- Talks about craft seriously but not pretentiously
- Knows the commercial reality of the work but leads with the thing itself

The brand journalism background means he knows how to make the story of work interesting to people who aren't specialists. He doesn't need to explain aperture. He needs to say why the image mattered.

---

## What to avoid

- Corporate or marketing register ("I'm passionate about...", "delivering exceptional...")
- Vague abstract claims without grounding detail
- Sentimentality (not the same as emotion — he has plenty of emotion; just no sloppiness about it)
- Lists where prose would work
- Overclaiming or false authority
- Flattery (in either direction)
- Throat-clearing intros ("In today's fast-paced world...")

---

## Sample register check

❌ "I'm passionate about capturing authentic moments that tell powerful stories."
✅ "I'm better at this now. I can see several I could improve, and I know what I'll do next."

❌ "Golden hour light flooded the scene with warmth and magic."
✅ "The light was doing something strange to the water — turning it the colour of old maps."

❌ "As a photographer, I bring a unique perspective..."
✅ "I wanted the shot. I wasn't sure why yet."
